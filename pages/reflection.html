This experience taught me that not understanding something initially does not mean I cannot learn it. Instead of relying entirely on AI-generated outputs, I used them to clarify concepts, debug code, and brainstorm ideas, while ensuring that I understood the underlying logic myself. As such, differences in opinions and working styles occasionally led to misunderstandings.   • •Commitment to Continuous Improvement and Growth: Moving forward, I aim to maintain this mindset of perseverance and avoid giving up easily when faced with difficulties. I will also continue to seek feedback from my peers and lecturers to further improve my work. Overall, this project taught me more than just pharmacy business, as it also supported my personal growth by encouraging me to become more resilient and step outside my comfort zone.

Understanding Pharmacy Operations and Business Models

  • •Seeing the Bigger Picture in Pharmacy Operations: I gained understanding that pharmacy operations involve more than just dispensing medications, as they function as an integrated system where automation, inventory management, and regulatory compliance must work together to ensure safe and reliable service delivery. This idea became apparent to me through Vendo Pharmacy, where the absence of front staffs revealed the complexity behind what initially seem like a simple task.
  • •Using Technology to Support: I learned that technology functions as a strategic enabler in pharmacy operations rather than a standalone solution, as automation must be supported by clear workflows, monitoring systems, and appropriate human intervention to effectively manage operational risks. This is why we adopted a human-in-the-loop approach when integrating AI into Vendo Pharmacy.
  • •Considering Everyone’s Perspective: In addition, I learned to approach decisions from multiple stakeholder perspectives, including patients, partners, and Vendo Pharmacy. Since different stakeholders have varying levels of understanding, I learned the importance of presenting ideas clearly, especially when developing the project website, to ensure information could be easily understood by users from diverse backgrounds.
  • •Power of Strong Partnerships: I also learned the importance of strategic partnerships and the key considerations involved. Collaborations with healthcare institutions (e.g. NUH) and suppliers (e.g. Hyphens Pharma) strengthen the business model by enhancing credibility, supply reliability, and overall service quality. As Vendo Pharmacy is a relatively new concept, operating alone could face challenges in gaining public trust, making this business idea impossible.
  • •Focusing on Safety, Trust, and Sustainability: I also gained the insight that the pharmacy business model is not about selling more medications to earn higher profits, but about designing operations that sustainably deliver convenience, safety, and trust. Even with strong demand, issues such as poor cost control, system downtime, or unclear communication can undermine the entire business model.
  • •Designing for Real People, Not Just Convenience: We faced challenges in setting up Vendo Pharmacy, as it is unlike normal vending machines that sell ordinary retail products. We had to consider patient safety, correct medication use, and the need to provide proper information before dispensing medicine. This made the design more complex, as features such as clear instructions, a safe user flow, and controlled access had to be carefully planned. Through this experience, I learned that technology solutions in healthcare must always prioritise safety, accuracy, and user understanding, not just convenience or speed.

Understanding Pharmacy Operations and Business Models

  • •Broadened my view of what pharmacy operations really mean: I realized that pharmacy services go way beyond dispensing and counselling. It is a whole system that keeps the pharmacy running such as inventory control, staff roles, replenishment planning, and partnerships that keep supply safe and steady. Because our model is a vending-machine pharmacy, we couldn’t hide behind a normal counter. We had to be unique and design a service people would actually trust and choose. So, we focused on being clear about our value: accessible 24/7, simple for common needs, and properly supported when a pharmacist is required.
  • •Changed the way I think about technology: Through this project, I saw the real value technology can bring, but also the risks it can introduce. At first, we added tech mainly to solve practical problems, like guiding users to choose suitable medication, providing counselling points, and improving inventory management. As we progressed, I realised that not every “smart” feature makes the service safer. Some features can increase risk if they are not controlled properly, especially in patient safety and compliance. This pushed me to think more critically about ethical boundaries, risk control, and what must remain pharmacist-led instead of automated.
  • •Reinforced the purpose of compliance: This project changed how I see compliance. It isn’t just something you add to satisfy requirements, it is what makes the model safe and believable. Compliance is what protects patients and builds trust, without it, convenience doesn’t matter. To keep our proposal defensible, we built clear boundaries, identity verification, and proper documentation. This helped me see that a credible business model is built around safety and accountability, not only speed.
  • •Developed a customer-journey mindset: Doing the PIRATE funnel and SWOT pushed me to think beyond a “good idea.” In healthcare, people hesitate because safety and trust matter. I started designing around the feelings behind behaviour, uncertainty, fear of choosing wrongly, and the need for reassurance. That is why I improved the content and touchpoints to guide users clearly and reduce friction, instead of assuming the machine alone would convince them.
  • •Reality check: When I worked on the revenue model, I felt the pressure of reality. Upfront costs and ongoing costs add up quickly, and breaking even takes time. That forced me to think beyond “medication sales” and if our whole ideas are totally feasible. This forced me to think about other revenue models, and this was where VendAI started forming naturally, not as a flashy add-on, but as a way to add value through decision support. Through revisions, I also learnt to clearly separate VendAI from pharmacist roles, which made our proposal safer and much more defensible
